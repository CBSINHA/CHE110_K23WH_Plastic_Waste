Plastic Waste

What is Plastic?

Plastic is a polymeric,synthetic material characterized by its malleable, versatile, and durable nature, integral to industrial, commercial, and domestic spheres. Originated from the polymerization of petrochemical-derived monomers, plastics are composed of long chains of repeating molecular units. This molecular structure imparts a unique combination of physical properties, including but not limited to flexibility, elasticity, and resistance to degradation. Plastics can be categorized into various types based on their composition, such as polyethylene, polypropylene, polyvinyl chloride and many others, each exhibiting distinct characteristics designed for specific applications.

The ubiquity of plastics in everyday life is underscored by their widespread use in packaging materials, consumer goods, textiles, automotive components, medical devices, and construction materials. The durability of plastics, while advantageous in certain contexts, has raised environmental concerns due to their persistence in ecosystems and contribution to pollution.

The Impact of Plastic Wastage

The impact of plastic wastage is far-reaching and multifaceted, casting a shadow over environmental, human health, and economic domains. One of the most alarming consequences is the ecological havoc wrought upon terrestrial and marine ecosystems. Plastics, being non-biodegradable, persist in the environment for extended periods, accumulating in landfills and polluting oceans. Marine life bears a heavy toll, with animals often ingesting or getting entangled in plastic debris, leading to injury, disruption of ecosystems, and even species endangerment. The proliferation of microplastics further intensifies the problem, infiltrating water sources and entering the food chain, posing potential risks to human health. Beyond the environmental realm, the economic impact is substantial, as communities and nations grapple with the costs of waste management, environmental cleanup, and the toll on industries such as tourism and fisheries which are mostly dependent on cleaner environment.

The impact on human health is a growing concern, as plastics and their byproducts introduce potentially harmful substances into the food we consume and the air we breathe. Chemical additives in plastics, along with the absorption of pollutants by plastic particles, raise questions about the long-term health effects on individuals and communities. Moreover, the economic fallout of plastic wastage is substantial, with the costs of waste management, environmental remediation, and healthcare expenditures escalating.

•Plastic Waste on Land

The impact of plastic waste on landscapes is both extensive and subtle, leaving a mark on the environment. Discarded plastic items, ranging from packaging materials to disposable products, find their way into landfills where they accumulate in vast quantities. These plastic-laden landfills not only present an unsightly blight on the landscape but also contribute to environmental degradation. As plastics break down over time, they release a load of harmful chemicals, posing a potential threat to the soil and groundwater. This chemical leaching can have serious effects on the quality of agricultural land, with potential consequences for crops and ecosystems. Moreover, the visible presence of plastic litter on the ground, whether in urban areas or remote areas, alters the aesthetics of natural landscapes and can disrupt the ecological balance. Wildlife on land is not spared from the impacts, as animals may consume or become entangled in plastic debris, leading to injuries or even their death.

•Plastic Waste in Water Bodies

The effects of plastic waste in water bodies are far-reaching and threatening to aquatic ecosystems. Discarded plastics, from single-use bottles to fishing equipments, often find their way into rivers, lakes, and oceans, causing widespread pollution. Aquatic life faces consequences as marine animals mistake plastic debris for food, leading to ingestion and often fatal consequences. The entanglement of marine creatures in plastic, such as abandoned fishing nets, further worsens the problem. The persistent nature of plastics in water leads to the fragmentation of larger items into microplastics, creating a pervasive and virtually invisible threat. These microplastics infiltrate the water column and sediment, posing risks to the health of aquatic organisms and potentially entering the food chain. The contamination of water bodies with plastic waste not only disrupts marine biodiversity but also threatens human health, as communities relying on these water sources for sustenance may be exposed to harmful pollutants.

•Plastic Waste in Atmosphere

While plastic waste primarily impacts terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems, it also indirectly affects the atmosphere through various processes. The production, burning and decomposition of plastics contribute to air pollution and greenhouse gas emissions. The manufacturing of plastic involves the extraction and processing of fossil fuels, releasing significant amounts of carbon dioxide and other pollutants into the atmosphere. Incinerating plastic waste further adds the problem, emitting harmful substances such as dioxins and particulate matter. Additionally, as plastics break down over time, they release volatile organic compounds (VOCs) into the air, contributing to air pollution.

How to Solve the Plastic Waste Problem

Due to the alarming rate of increase of plastic everywhere around the world and the harmful nature of plastic in the environment,it is not only the government's responsibility or some organisation's responsibility to control the pollution caused by plastic wastage but it is also our responsibilities as individuals to check on the amount of plastic we dispose off on a regular basis.

Here are some of the ways using which we can solve the plastic wastage problems:

  • Prevent buying disposable plastic cutlery, plastic straws and other single-use plastics.
  • Avoid plastics that cannot be recycled if other alternatives exist.
  • Avoid products with excess or unnecessary plastic packaging.
  • Adopt reusable items such as water bottles, shopping bags, keep cups and travel cutlery.
  • Replace plastic tupperware for glass or steel containers.
  • Avoid cosmetics that use micro-plastics, buy biodegradable brush and natural fabrics.
  • Buy in bulk to reduce packaging waste.
  • Recycle.

Common Misconceptions About Plastic

  • All plastics are non biodegradable:Yes,most plastics are a concern for our environment and are not biodegradable but some plastics like Cellulose based plastics,Lignin-based polymer composites and many more can actually decompose.
  • All plastics are harmful:Yes plastic pollution is a significant concern but plastics can actually be helpful as they contribute in various industries like medical, technological and safety applications.
  • Plastics labeled as 'BPA-free' are completely safe:BPA-free plastics may contain alternative chemicals which can cause other health effects.
  • Plastics last forever and don't decompose:This is a very common misconception among people that plastics won't decompose.Plastics take hundreds to a thousand years to break down and they eventually degrade.
  • All plastic packaging is unnecessary: While some packaging is unnecessary not all plastic packagings are unnecessary, sometimes the packaging improves self-life of a product.
